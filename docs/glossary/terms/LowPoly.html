Low Poly Mesh

Definition Overview:

The term "Low Poly" refers to the "Game Res Mesh", (also sometimes called the "Render Mesh" or "Arbitrary Mesh". A mesh that is "Low" in polygonal count/density (no unnecessary faces/verts/edges). This is the mesh you actually use "In-Game" and it's topology is decided not by edge flow or "SubD rules" but instead solely by the needs of the video game asset. The only edges/verts/polys kept are there to support silhouette, shading, UVs, other game data... every poly/vert is there to serve one of those purposes, and nothing "extra" is left as that would be wasteful can cost both memory and runtime performance. 

This mesh more than likely would consist of triangles to ensure it's efficient. 99% of the time when done well, this mesh is NOT the same thing as the mesh you subdivide to make your "SubD High Poly" ("Base Cage", Base Mesh", i.e. Lowest Subdivision in ZBrush or non-"smooth mesh preview" mesh in Maya). 
A related term is a "Mid-Poly". While **I** personally still consider such a mesh simply "a higher poly count "Low Poly"..." and don't care for the term "Mid Poly" much, it is primarily used to distinguish a mesh that does NOT have a normal map baked from a "High Poly", and instead used more edges/verts to help keep it shading and looking the way the artist wants while relying on tiling textures and trim sheets instead of a uniquely baked normal map from a "High Poly" mesh. A "Mid Poly" is a game res mesh that usually has more polys than a "Low Poly" because it uses trim sheets and tiling/world textures instead of the "High Poly/Low Poly Bake Workflow"). 
More information at: https://pixune.com/blog/high-poly-to-low-poly-workflow-in-game-art/
